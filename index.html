Core Idea

What is density?

Density tells us how much mass is packed into a certain volume. It helps explain why two objects of the same size can feel very different in weight.

Big idea

A material has high density when a lot of mass is packed into a small space. A material has low density when the same space contains less mass.

density mass / volume

Particle view

Low density
High density

Closely packed particles usually mean higher density. More spread out particles usually mean lower density.

Same size, different mass

A small metal cube can feel heavier than a large sponge because metal packs more mass into each cubic centimetre.

Common units

In school labs density is often measured in g/cm3. In larger science problems, kg/m3 is also common.

Misconception

Heavy objects do not always sink. Floating depends on density compared with the liquid, not just total mass.

Worked example

Mass = 120 g

Volume = 40 cm3

Density = 120 / 40 = 3 g/cm3

Divide mass by volume.

Finding volume with a meniscus

Irregular objects do not have neat sides, so their volume is found by measuring how much water they displace in a measuring cylinder.

Read the volume at the bottom of the curved meniscus, not the top.

Watch the object lower into the water, then choose the correct place to read.

  1. Pour water into a measuring cylinder.
  2. Read the initial volume at the bottom of the meniscus.
  3. Carefully place the irregular object into the water.
  4. Read the final volume at the bottom of the meniscus.
  5. Calculate object volume = final volume - initial volume.

Worked example

Initial volume = 40 cm3

Final volume = 65 cm3

Object volume = 65 - 40 = 25 cm3 Remember: 1 ml = 1 cm3

Estimate both readings from the bottom of the meniscus.

Use final volume minus initial volume.
